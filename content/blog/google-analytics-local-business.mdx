# Google Analytics 4 for Local Service Businesses: What You Need to Track

You installed Google Analytics on your website because someone told you it was important. Maybe your web developer set it up. Maybe you followed a YouTube tutorial.

Now what?

If you're like most local service business owners, you log in occasionally, stare at a dashboard full of charts you don't fully understand, and leave after 30 seconds because nothing seems actionable.

That's not your fault. Google Analytics 4 (GA4) was designed for enterprise e-commerce companies with data analysts on staff — not for HVAC contractors, plumbers, and electricians who just want to know "is my website actually bringing in business?"

Here's the good news: there are exactly five metrics that matter for local service businesses. Everything else is noise.

## The Five Metrics That Actually Matter

### 1. Phone Call Conversions

For service businesses, the phone call is the most important conversion event on your website. A visitor can browse your pages, read your content, and look at your photos — but if they don't call you, none of it matters.

GA4 can track phone calls in two ways:

**Click-to-call tracking:** When a mobile user taps your phone number, GA4 records it as a conversion event. This requires proper setup of your phone number as a clickable `tel:` link.

**Call tracking numbers:** A dedicated phone number on your website that forwards to your real business line. Every call to this number is tracked — not just clicks, but actual calls, including duration and outcome.

What to look for:
- **Calls per week/month** — is your call volume trending up or down?
- **Call duration** — calls under 30 seconds are usually wrong numbers. Calls over 2 minutes are real leads.
- **Calls by source** — which channel (Google Search, Google Maps, paid ad, social media) drives the most calls?

Most service businesses find that **Google Business Profile and organic search drive 60-70% of all phone calls**. If your analytics show otherwise, something is misconfigured.

### 2. Form Submissions

Contact forms are your second most important conversion point. But most GA4 setups don't track form submissions properly — they track "page views" on a thank-you page, which misses everyone who starts a form but doesn't finish.

Proper form tracking in GA4 measures:
- **Form impressions** — how many people see the form
- **Form starts** — how many people click into the first field
- **Form completions** — successful submissions
- **Form abandonment rate** — the percentage who start but don't finish

A high abandonment rate (over 60%) usually means your form is too long, asks for too much information, or has technical issues.

The sweet spot for service business contact forms: **3-5 fields** (Name, Phone, Service Needed, Message, optional Email). Every additional field costs you conversions.

### 3. Google Business Profile Engagement

Here's something most business owners miss: GA4 can't directly track GBP interactions, but you can track what happens *after* someone visits your Google listing.

Use UTM parameters on your GBP links:
- `?utm_source=google&utm_medium=gbp&utm_campaign=listing`
- `?utm_source=google&utm_medium=gbp&utm_campaign=service-page`

This tells GA4 exactly how many website visitors came from your Google Business Profile, what they did on your site, and whether they converted.

Cross-reference this with Google Business Profile Insights:
- **Views** — how many people saw your listing
- **Searches** — how many people searched for your business
- **Actions** — website clicks, direction requests, and phone calls

If your GBP gets 500 views but only 10 website clicks, your listing has a visibility problem. If it gets 100 clicks but 0 conversions, your website has a conversion problem. GA4 helps you distinguish between the two.

### 4. Traffic Sources

Where are your website visitors actually coming from? GA4 breaks this down into default channels:

| Channel | What It Means | Typical % for Service Businesses |
|---------|--------------|----------------------------------|
| Organic Search | Google/Bing search results | 35-50% |
| Direct | Typed URL or bookmark | 15-25% |
| Paid Search | Google Ads | 10-20% |
| Social Media | Facebook, Instagram, Nextdoor | 5-10% |
| Referral | Other websites linking to you | 3-8% |
| Email | Newsletters, campaigns | 2-5% |

The most important insight: **organic search should be your dominant channel.** If paid search or social media is outperforming organic, you have an SEO problem.

For local service businesses, we also look at "source/medium" breakdown within organic:
- **google / organic** — regular Google search
- **google / organic** with landing page = GBP → you're getting traffic from your Google profile
- **bing / organic** — often overlooked but still drives 5-10% of traffic for older demographics

### 5. User Behavior Flow

This is the most underutilized feature in GA4. The User Behavior Flow report shows the path visitors take through your website:

- Where they enter (landing page)
- Which pages they visit next
- Where they drop off (leave your site)

For service businesses, the ideal flow is:
1. **Landing page** (usually your homepage or a service page)
2. **Service page** (HVAC repair, plumbing, electrical, etc.)
3. **Contact page** (call or form submission)

If your users are following a different path (e.g., hitting the blog and leaving, or bouncing from the homepage), your site structure needs work.

**What to fix based on behavior flow:**

- **High homepage bounce rate (>70%)** — your homepage doesn't clearly communicate what you do or why visitors should stay
- **Service page exits** — visitors read about your services but don't proceed to contact you. Your service pages need stronger calls-to-action
- **About page attraction** — if lots of traffic goes to your About page instead of Service pages, your navigation is confusing
- **Blog-only visitors** — if people only visit your blog and leave, you need better internal links from blog posts to service pages

## Setting Up GA4 Properly (The Minimalist Version)

Most local service businesses overcomplicate their GA4 setup. Here's what you actually need:

### Step 1: Install GA4 Correctly

Don't use a plugin that "integrates" GA4 in a way that strips out important data. Use Google Tag Manager or direct tag installation. Verify it's working with the GA4 DebugView or the Tag Assistant browser extension. Follow our [Google Tag Manager guide](/blog/google-tag-manager-hvac) for step-by-step installation instructions.

### Step 2: Set Up Conversion Events

Configure these as conversions in GA4:
- `phone_call_click` — when someone taps your phone number on mobile
- `form_submission` — when someone completes your contact form
- `email_click` — when someone clicks your email address
- `direction_request` — if you have a "Get Directions" link

### Step 3: Enable Enhanced Measurement

GA4's Enhanced Measurement automatically tracks:
- Page views
- Scrolls (when someone scrolls 90% of a page)
- Outbound clicks
- Site search
- Video engagement

Turn all of these on. They're free data points that provide valuable insights.

### Step 4: Create a Custom Dashboard

Build a dashboard in GA4 that shows only the five metrics above. Hide everything else. This prevents data overwhelm and keeps you focused on what matters.

### Step 5: Set Up Google Search Console Integration

Link GA4 to Google Search Console. This gives you search query data — the actual keywords people type before finding your website. This is invaluable for understanding what services people are searching for in your area.

## Common GA4 Mistakes Local Businesses Make

### Mistake 1: Not Filtering Internal Traffic

If you and your employees visit your own website regularly, GA4 counts those visits as "traffic." Without filtering your own IP address, your analytics are inflated. Set up an internal traffic filter in GA4 using your office IP address.

### Mistake 2: Relying on Default Reporting

GA4's default reports are designed for everyone — which means they're optimized for no one. Custom reports for local service businesses show entirely different patterns than generic reports.

### Mistake 3: Ignoring Engagement Rate

GA4 measures "engaged sessions" — sessions that last longer than 10 seconds, have a conversion event, or include 2+ page views. Your engagement rate should be above 50%. Below that means visitors aren't finding what they need.

### Mistake 4: No Conversion Value

GA4 lets you assign monetary values to conversions. If your average service call generates $400 in revenue, set your `phone_call_click` conversion value to $40 (10% close rate assumption). This lets GA4 estimate the revenue your website generates — a powerful number to track monthly.

# Why Inconsistent Business Listings Are Killing Your Local SEO (And How to Fix It)

If you run an HVAC, plumbing, electrical, or roofing business in Texas, your business information is scattered across more than a dozen online directories. Yelp. Facebook. Bing. Apple Maps. Yellow Pages. Angi. Nextdoor. Foursquare. Better Business Bureau. And that's just the start.

Here's the problem: **most of those listings have wrong information.**

Maybe your old phone number is still showing on Yelp. Your Facebook page has a different address than your website. Apple Maps routes customers to a location you moved away from two years ago.

Each inconsistency by itself seems small. But together, they're quietly destroying your local search rankings — and costing you customers every single day.

## What Is NAP Consistency and Why Should You Care?

NAP stands for **Name, Address, Phone** — the three core pieces of information that identify your business online. NAP consistency means every single directory on the internet shows exactly the same information.

When Google sees your business listed as "Sims Investment Management Services LLC" on one site and "Sims Investment Management" on another, it has a problem. Which one is real? Which customers have actually visited? Google's algorithm detects these conflicting signals and does the only logical thing: **it downgrades your trust score.**

The result is brutally simple. You rank lower in local search results. Fewer people find your business. Fewer calls. Fewer jobs.

Think of NAP consistency like a reference check. If you applied for a job and three of your references described you completely differently, the employer wouldn't know what to believe. Google operates the same way — conflicting data is treated as unreliable data.

## How Google Detects Conflicting Signals

Google's local search algorithm is smarter than most business owners realize. Here's what it looks for:

**Citation consistency.** Google cross-references your NAP across the entire web. If 12 out of 15 directories agree but 3 show different information, Google notices. It treats the minority listings as potential "noise" that weakens the signal. The more conflicting signals exist, the less confident Google becomes in showing your business in search results.

**Proximity signals.** When a customer searches "plumber near me," Google needs to know exactly where you are. Conflicting addresses confuse this calculation. Your business may not show up for searches it should rank for because Google isn't sure where you're actually located. A wrong address on even a few directories can push you out of the local 3-pack.

**Trust signals.** A business that can't keep its own directory information straight sends a negative trust signal. Google's algorithm treats inconsistent NAP data similarly to how it treats expired SSL certificates or broken website links — as evidence of a neglected online presence. And if you've neglected your basic business information, Google reasons, you're probably not delivering a great customer experience either.

**Duplicate listings.** When the same business appears twice with slightly different names or addresses, Google doesn't know which one to show. Sometimes it shows both — splitting your review count, traffic, and ranking signals across two weaker profiles instead of one strong one. We've seen businesses with 40 reviews split across two duplicate profiles, making both look weak compared to a competitor with 25 reviews on a single, clean profile.

## How Data Aggregators Propagate Errors

Here's a hidden problem most business owners don't know about: data aggregators.

Companies like Infogroup, Neustar (formerly Localeze), Factual, and Acxiom serve as the backbone of directory data. They distribute your business information to hundreds of directories, apps, and GPS systems. When your information is wrong in these aggregators, the error spreads automatically.

This is why fixing NAP on Yelp and Facebook isn't enough. If the underlying data in an aggregator is wrong, it will keep re-propagating the error to other platforms over time. You might fix Yelp today, and a data sync from an aggregator overwrites your correction next month.

**The fix:** Claim your listing on the major data aggregators directly. This establishes your information as the authoritative source and prevents automatic overwrites.

## A Step-by-Step Fix for NAP Inconsistencies

Fixing your directory listings isn't complicated, but it takes focused effort. Here's exactly what to do:

### Step 1: Run an Audit

Start by claiming or verifying your business on the 18 platforms listed above. For each one, record exactly what Name, Address, and Phone is currently showing. Create a simple spreadsheet with columns for each platform and the three NAP fields. Note which listings are missing entirely.

### Step 2: Standardize Your NAP

Before you fix anything, decide on the authoritative version of your information. This must match **exactly** what's on your website — down to the abbreviation (use "Street" not "St.", "Suite" not "Ste.", etc.). Your website is the source of truth.

**Pro tip:** Google prefers the format that matches what's on your website footer. If your site says "1200 Main Street, Suite 100" — that's what every directory should show. Don't abbreviate on some and spell out on others.

### Step 3: Fix Every Platform

Go through your spreadsheet and correct every discrepancy. Most platforms make this straightforward through their business dashboard. Some (like Apple Maps) require a verification process. Others (like Foursquare) let you update directly.

### Step 4: Claim Missing Listings

For any platform where your business isn't listed at all, create a new listing. Use your standardized NAP information. Complete every field they offer — categories, hours, services, photos. A complete listing performs better than a barebones one on every platform.

### Step 5: Remove Duplicate Listings

If you find the same business on a platform multiple times, contact their support to merge or remove duplicates. This consolidates your ranking signals into one authoritative profile.

### Step 6: Monitor Continuously

Directory listings aren't a set-it-and-forget-it fix. Data aggregators share information between platforms, and one change can create a cascade of inconsistencies. Run a fresh audit quarterly. Set calendar reminders. NAP consistency requires ongoing attention.
